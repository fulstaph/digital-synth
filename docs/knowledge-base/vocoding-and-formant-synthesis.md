# Vocoding And Formant Synthesis

Vocoding and formant synthesis create voice-like or vocal-influenced timbres. The vocoder imposes the spectral shape of one sound onto another, while formant synthesis generates vocal characteristics directly from parameter control. Both techniques add a distinctly human quality to synthetic sound. They occupy different points on a spectrum of vocal emulation: the vocoder depends on an external voice signal for articulation, while formant synthesis produces vowel-like qualities entirely from internal parameters. Together they represent the primary tools for bridging the gap between electronic timbres and the human voice.

## Vocoder

A vocoder analyzes the spectral shape of one signal, called the modulator, and imposes that shape onto another signal, called the carrier. The modulator is typically a voice or microphone input that provides the articulation and rhythm of speech. The carrier is typically a synthesizer tone that provides the pitch and harmonic content. The result sounds like the synthesizer is speaking, because the rapid spectral changes of human speech are transferred onto the harmonic material of the carrier.

The process works because human speech is defined primarily by its spectral envelope rather than by its raw waveform. When a person speaks, the vocal tract shapes the spectrum of the voice into patterns that the listener interprets as vowels, consonants, and syllables. The vocoder captures these spectral patterns from the modulator and forces the carrier to adopt the same patterns. The carrier's own harmonic content fills in the spectral shape, so the listener hears the carrier's pitch and texture articulated by the modulator's speech rhythm.

The vocoder creates one of the most iconic effects in electronic music. Its sound is immediately recognizable and has appeared across decades of electronic, pop, and experimental music. Beyond its creative value, the vocoder demonstrates a fundamental principle of signal processing: that the spectral envelope of a sound can be separated from its fine harmonic structure and recombined with different material. This principle is relevant to many other synthesis and processing techniques.

The carrier signal must contain enough harmonic content for the vocoder to work effectively. A harmonically rich waveform like a sawtooth provides spectral energy across a wide frequency range, giving the vocoder material to shape at every band. A sine wave, by contrast, contains energy at only one frequency and produces thin, unintelligible vocoder output because most bands have nothing to work with. Noise can also serve as an effective carrier, producing a whispered or breathy vocoder effect because noise contains energy at all frequencies but without harmonic structure.

The quality of the modulator signal also matters. A clear, close-miked voice with minimal background noise produces the best results because the vocoder can extract clean spectral envelope information. Noisy or reverberant modulator signals blur the spectral analysis, reducing intelligibility and producing a muddier output. Preprocessing the modulator with compression or gating can improve vocoder clarity.

The vocoder can also be used with non-vocal modulators. A drum loop as a modulator imposes rhythmic amplitude patterns onto the carrier, creating a synth sound that pulses and breathes with the drum pattern. An acoustic guitar as a modulator transfers the strumming dynamics and harmonic articulation onto the carrier. These non-vocal applications demonstrate that the vocoder is fundamentally a spectral transfer tool, not strictly a voice effect, and expands the creative range significantly beyond the classic robot voice.

Polyphonic vocoding adds another dimension by allowing the carrier to play chords while the modulator articulates. When the carrier is a polyphonic synthesizer producing a chord, the vocoded output sounds like a choir or ensemble speaking in harmony. Each note in the chord receives the same spectral envelope from the modulator, creating the impression of multiple voices articulating together. This is one of the most musically powerful vocoder applications and relies on the carrier providing sufficient harmonic content across multiple simultaneous pitches.

The vocoder's behavior also depends on the fundamental pitch relationship between the modulator and carrier. When the carrier pitch is in the same range as the modulator voice, the result tends to sound most natural and intelligible. When the carrier pitch is very different from the modulator's fundamental, the spectral envelope mapping becomes less perceptually coherent because the harmonic structure of the carrier does not align with the spectral peaks of the modulator. This misalignment is not necessarily a problem for creative use, but it explains why some carrier pitch ranges produce clearer vocoder effects than others.

Latency is an important consideration for live vocoder performance. The analysis process introduces a small delay because the envelope followers need time to measure the modulator's amplitude in each band. Shorter analysis windows reduce latency but also reduce the accuracy of the spectral measurement, creating a tradeoff between responsiveness and fidelity. For real-time performance with a microphone, the total latency through the vocoder path should remain low enough that the performer does not perceive a distracting delay between speaking and hearing the vocoded output. Keeping analysis window lengths short and processing efficiently is essential for a musically responsive vocoder.

## Vocoder Band Structure

The vocoder works by splitting both the modulator and carrier into matching frequency bands. For each band, the vocoder measures the amplitude of the modulator and applies that amplitude to the corresponding carrier band. The result is a carrier whose spectral shape follows the modulator's spectral shape, band by band.

The number of bands determines how precisely the vocoder can reproduce the modulator's spectral detail. A vocoder with eight bands divides the frequency range into eight broad regions. Each region captures an average of the spectral activity within its range but cannot distinguish fine details. A vocoder with thirty-two bands divides the frequency range much more finely, capturing subtler spectral movements and producing more intelligible speech reproduction.

Band count affects intelligibility because speech relies on spectral detail in the 1 to 4 kilohertz range. This region contains the formant transitions and consonant energy that distinguish one syllable from another. With too few bands covering this range, the vocoder cannot resolve these critical distinctions. Vowels may still come through because they involve broad spectral peaks, but consonants and transitions between sounds are lost, making the output sound more like a vague tonal wash than recognizable speech.

Fewer bands produce a smoother, more overtly synthetic effect that can be musically desirable even when intelligibility suffers. Many classic vocoder sounds use relatively low band counts, creating a thick, blurred vocal texture that emphasizes the tonal quality of the carrier over the clarity of the modulator's speech. Higher band counts sound more natural and intelligible but can also sound clinical or less characteristically electronic.

Band spacing is another important design consideration. Equal spacing across the frequency range does not match how human hearing works. Logarithmic or perceptually motivated spacing, where bands are narrower at low frequencies and wider at high frequencies, better matches the resolution of human pitch perception. Some vocoders use bark-scale or mel-scale band spacing to align with auditory perception, improving the perceptual quality of the output.

Each band requires its own envelope follower to track the modulator's amplitude and its own gain stage to apply that amplitude to the carrier. The speed of the envelope followers determines how quickly the vocoder responds to changes in the modulator. Fast envelope followers track rapid consonant articulations but may also track unwanted noise. Slow envelope followers create a smoother, more legato vocal effect but lose crisp articulation.

The computational cost of a vocoder scales directly with band count. Each band requires a pair of band-pass filters (one for the modulator analysis, one for the carrier shaping), an envelope follower, and a gain multiplier. A thirty-two-band vocoder therefore requires sixty-four band-pass filters and thirty-two envelope followers per voice. This cost is manageable for a single vocoder effect applied to a summed output, but becomes significant if the vocoder is intended to operate per-voice in a polyphonic context. The architecture should account for whether vocoding occurs before or after voice summation.

Some vocoders add a sibilance or noise channel that operates alongside the main band structure. This channel detects high-frequency noise content in the modulator, typically corresponding to unvoiced consonants like "s" and "sh," and mixes filtered noise into the carrier output when these sounds are detected. Without this channel, a vocoder loses crisp consonant articulation and the output sounds exclusively vowel-driven. Including even a simple noise detection channel significantly improves the perceived clarity and naturalness of vocoded speech.

The filter type used for each band affects the vocoder's character. Steep filters with high rolloff create sharp separation between bands, producing a more defined and analytical sound. Gentler filters allow more overlap between adjacent bands, creating a smoother and more blended character. The choice of filter slope is a design decision that shapes the overall personality of the vocoder. Some vocoders offer adjustable filter slopes as a timbral control, while others commit to a fixed filter design that defines the instrument's sonic identity.

The frequency range covered by the vocoder bands also matters. Speech contains relevant spectral information from roughly 100 hertz to 8 kilohertz, but the most important region for intelligibility is the 300 hertz to 5 kilohertz range. Bands that extend below 200 hertz capture the fundamental frequency of the voice, adding a sense of body and weight to the vocoder output. Bands above 5 kilohertz capture the air and presence of sibilant sounds. A vocoder whose band range is too narrow will miss either the low-end weight or the high-end clarity, limiting the quality of the result regardless of band count.

## Formants

Formants are resonant frequency peaks in the acoustic spectrum that define vowel sounds. They arise from the resonant properties of the vocal tract: the shape and size of the throat, mouth, and nasal cavities create standing waves at specific frequencies, amplifying certain parts of the spectrum while attenuating others.

Each vowel sound has a characteristic pattern of formant positions. The first formant, F1, typically falls between roughly 300 and 800 hertz and correlates primarily with the openness of the mouth. Open vowels like "ah" have a high F1, while closed vowels like "ee" have a low F1. The second formant, F2, typically falls between roughly 800 and 2500 hertz and correlates with the front-to-back position of the tongue. Front vowels like "ee" have a high F2, while back vowels like "oo" have a low F2. The third formant and beyond contribute to the individual character and naturalness of the voice but are less critical for basic vowel identification.

The positions of formants determine which vowel we perceive regardless of the fundamental pitch being spoken or sung. A singer can sing "ah" on a low note and the same "ah" on a high note, and the formant positions remain similar even though the fundamental frequency changes dramatically. This independence of formant position from pitch is a critical acoustic property. It means that vocal character is defined by resonance structure rather than by the raw oscillation frequency of the vocal cords.

Formants matter for synthesis because they are the acoustic basis of vowel perception and vocal character. Any synthesis method that can create peaks at the right frequencies can produce sounds that the listener perceives as vowel-like. This is true whether the peaks come from band-pass filters, resonators, spectral shaping, or physical modeling of a vocal tract. The formant concept connects the physics of resonance to the psychology of vowel recognition.

Understanding formants also explains why certain filter settings on a subtractive synthesizer can produce vowel-like qualities. A resonant band-pass filter swept through the formant region can hint at vocal sounds. Stacking two or three resonant filters at different frequencies can produce convincing vowel approximations. This is not an accident but a direct consequence of the formant principle.

Formant bandwidth also plays a role in vocal realism. Each formant peak is not infinitely narrow but has a characteristic width that affects how much of the surrounding spectrum it amplifies. Narrow formant bandwidths produce sharp, resonant peaks that can sound nasal or synthetic. Wider bandwidths produce broader, gentler peaks that sound more natural and relaxed. The bandwidth of each formant varies between vowels and between different vocal types, adding another dimension to accurate vocal modeling.

Beyond vowels, formants also contribute to the perception of consonants, vocal register, and individual voice identity. The spacing and relative amplitudes of higher formants (F3, F4, F5) distinguish one person's voice from another even when both are producing the same vowel at the same pitch. While these higher formants are less critical for basic synthesis applications, they become important when the goal is realistic vocal emulation rather than stylized vocal color.

The relationship between formants and the harmonic series of the source pitch creates interesting perceptual effects at different pitch ranges. At low pitches, where harmonics are closely spaced, the formant peaks have many harmonics to amplify, producing a full and well-defined vowel sound. At high pitches, where harmonics are widely spaced, the formant peaks may contain only one or two harmonics each, making the vowel less distinct and more ambiguous. This is why sopranos singing at the top of their range can be harder to understand than lower voices: the wide harmonic spacing provides less spectral detail for the formants to shape. For a synthesizer, this means that formant-based patches may need adjustment when transposed across a wide pitch range to maintain consistent vowel clarity.

## Formant Synthesis

Formant synthesis generates vocal timbres without requiring a voice input. Instead of analyzing an external signal to extract spectral shape, formant synthesis constructs the desired spectral shape directly from parameter values. Parallel band-pass filters or resonators are tuned to specific formant frequencies, and their combined output imposes a vowel-like spectral envelope onto whatever source signal feeds them.

The basic approach uses two or more band-pass filters in parallel, each tuned to a formant frequency. For the vowel "ah," the first filter might be centered near 700 hertz and the second near 1200 hertz. For "ee," the first filter might be near 300 hertz and the second near 2700 hertz. By defining sets of formant positions for different vowels, the synthesizer can switch between or morph through vowel shapes.

Morphing between vowels is achieved by smoothly interpolating the filter center frequencies between one vowel's formant positions and another's. Moving from "ah" to "ee" means sliding F1 from 700 toward 300 hertz while sliding F2 from 1200 toward 2700 hertz. The intermediate positions produce transitional vowel sounds, creating the impression of a voice moving between syllables.

Formant synthesis creates vocal timbres from any harmonically rich source without needing a microphone or recorded voice. A sawtooth oscillator fed through formant filters produces a recognizable vowel sound. A pulse wave produces a different vocal character with the same vowel shape. Even noise fed through formant filters produces whispery vowel sounds. This versatility makes formant synthesis accessible as a timbral tool within any synthesizer that has a capable filter section.

The quality of formant synthesis depends on the bandwidth and resonance of the filters, the number of formant peaks modeled, and how accurately the formant positions match real vocal data. Two-formant synthesis produces recognizable but crude vowels. Three or more formants add realism and distinguish between similar vowels more effectively. Adding amplitude differences between formants, where some peaks are louder than others for a given vowel, further improves realism.

Formant synthesis is well suited to modulation. Because the vowel shape is defined by a small number of frequency parameters, those parameters can be driven by LFOs, envelopes, sequencers, or performance controls. An LFO sweeping between two vowel positions creates a rhythmic "wah" effect. A mod wheel mapped to a vowel morph axis gives the performer real-time control over the vocal character of the sound.

A vowel sequencer is a natural extension of formant synthesis. By defining a sequence of vowel positions and stepping through them in time with a clock or note trigger, the synthesizer can produce rhythmic vocal patterns that sound like a voice articulating syllables. This technique is common in electronic dance music, where a synthesizer pad cycles through vowel shapes to create a chanting or singing effect without any vocal recording.

The interaction between the source waveform and the formant filters deserves attention. Different waveforms produce different vocal qualities even with identical formant settings. A sawtooth wave, with its complete harmonic series, produces the fullest vowel sound because every harmonic is available for the formant filters to amplify. A square wave, missing even harmonics, produces a hollower vocal quality. A pulse wave with variable width allows the harmonic content to change continuously, enabling the vocal character to shift from full to hollow within a single note. Choosing the right source waveform is as important as setting the correct formant positions.

Formant synthesis also interacts meaningfully with the amplitude envelope of the voice. In natural speech, the spectral shape changes dynamically as the vocal tract moves between positions. An envelope that sweeps formant positions during the attack phase of a note can simulate the way a voice enters a vowel sound, starting from a neutral position and arriving at the target vowel. This attack-phase formant movement adds a naturalness that static formant positions cannot achieve.

The gain structure of parallel formant filters requires careful management. When multiple resonant filters operate in parallel, their outputs sum together at the mixing point. If all filters are set to high resonance, the combined output can be significantly louder than the input, especially when the source signal contains energy at all formant frequencies simultaneously. Automatic gain compensation that adjusts the output level based on the number of active formant filters and their resonance settings prevents unexpected volume spikes and makes formant synthesis safer to use in a live performance context.

Combining formant synthesis with other filter modes creates hybrid vocal textures. Running the output of formant filters through a subsequent low-pass filter allows the brightness of the vowel sound to be shaped independently of the vowel identity. Adding a high-pass filter before the formant stage removes low-frequency content that might muddy the vowel definition. These combinations demonstrate that formant synthesis integrates naturally into a flexible filter routing architecture rather than existing in isolation. The more routing options the filter section provides, the more expressive the formant synthesis capabilities become.

## Formant Shifting

Formant shifting moves the positions of all formant peaks up or down in frequency independently of the fundamental pitch. This decouples the resonant character of the sound from the note being played, creating effects that range from subtle timbral adjustment to dramatic vocal transformation.

Raising all formant positions while keeping the fundamental pitch constant makes a sound seem smaller, thinner, or more child-like. The spectral envelope moves into a higher range where the resonant peaks suggest a smaller vocal tract, which the listener associates with a smaller body or a higher-pitched speaker. The effect is immediate and recognizable: the pitch stays the same, but the voice quality shifts upward.

Lowering all formant positions while keeping the fundamental pitch constant produces the opposite effect. The sound seems larger, deeper, or more resonant. The spectral peaks suggest a larger vocal tract, evoking a larger body or a deeper-voiced speaker. This can add weight and authority to a synthetic vocal sound or create effects ranging from subtle darkening to monstrous growl.

The perceptual effect of formant shifting is remarkably strong because the human auditory system is highly attuned to vocal characteristics. Listeners involuntarily associate formant positions with physical attributes of the speaker, including body size, age, and gender. This deep perceptual association means that even small formant shifts on a synthetic sound can change the emotional quality of a patch. A bright pad with slightly raised formants can feel more intimate or youthful. The same pad with lowered formants can feel more authoritative or ominous. This makes formant shifting a powerful expressive tool that operates at a level below conscious analysis.

Formant shifting matters for creative sound design because it opens a space of vocal textures that do not exist in natural speech. A deep bass note with child-like formant positions creates an uncanny combination. A high-pitched sound with very low formants suggests something massive singing in a falsetto. These combinations are impossible in acoustic vocal production and give the synthesizer access to a uniquely electronic palette of vocal sounds.

Practical applications include gender-bending effects, where formant shifting creates the impression of a different vocal type without changing pitch. Alien or creature voice design relies heavily on formant shifting combined with pitch manipulation. Subtle formant adjustment can add presence or warmth to a vocal pad without changing its fundamental character. Extreme formant shifting produces textures that transcend vocal association entirely and enter abstract timbral territory.

Formant shifting interacts powerfully with other modulation sources. An envelope that shifts formants down during the attack and then back up during the sustain phase creates a vocal sound that starts deep and opens up, mimicking the way a singer might approach a note. An LFO applied to formant shift produces a vibrato-like wobble in the vocal character rather than in pitch, which is a distinctly different and less familiar effect. Velocity-controlled formant shift allows louder notes to sound brighter or more open, mapping performance dynamics to timbral response in a way that feels natural and expressive.

The amount of formant shift is typically expressed in semitones or as a frequency ratio. Small shifts of a few semitones create subtle character changes. Shifts of an octave or more produce extreme transformations. The parameter should be bipolar, allowing both upward and downward shifting from the natural position, and should be available as a modulation destination for dynamic formant movement during a note or performance.

Formant shifting can also be applied independently to individual formant peaks rather than shifting all formants uniformly. Moving F1 up while keeping F2 stationary produces different results than shifting both together. This independent control allows the creation of vowel shapes that do not correspond to any natural vocal production, opening a space of synthetic vocal textures that go beyond imitation into original timbral territory. However, independent formant control adds interface complexity and is best treated as an advanced option behind a simpler uniform shift control.

Keyboard tracking of formant shift is another useful control dimension. In natural singing, formants shift subtly as the singer moves across their range. Applying a small amount of automatic formant shift that tracks the played note can maintain a consistent vocal character across the keyboard. Without this tracking, a formant patch designed for the middle register may sound unnatural at the extremes of the keyboard because the relationship between the fundamental frequency and the formant positions changes. Providing keyboard tracking as an adjustable parameter lets users choose between consistent vocal character and the deliberate strangeness of fixed formants across a wide pitch range.

## Common Mistakes

Using a carrier with too few harmonics is the most frequent vocoder mistake. A sine wave carrier contains energy at only one frequency, so the vocoder can only shape one band. The result is thin and unintelligible regardless of how good the modulator signal is. Sawtooth waves, pulse waves, and noise are far more effective carriers because they provide spectral energy for the vocoder to shape across its entire band range. Users who are new to vocoders often try a sine carrier first and conclude the vocoder is broken when the real issue is carrier selection.

Setting too few vocoder bands and expecting speech intelligibility is a related mistake. Eight bands can produce musically useful effects, but they cannot reproduce the spectral detail needed for clear speech. Users who want recognizable words need sixteen bands or more, with good coverage in the 1 to 4 kilohertz range. Communicating this tradeoff between band count and intelligibility helps users choose the right setting for their intent rather than being disappointed by a fundamental limitation.

Confusing formant position with pitch is a conceptual error that leads to misunderstanding of both vocoders and formant synthesis. Formants are resonant peaks determined by filter positions or vocal tract shape. Pitch is determined by the fundamental oscillation frequency. They are independent parameters. Changing pitch does not change formants, and changing formants does not change pitch. Users who conflate the two will misinterpret what formant controls are doing and will not understand why a pitch shift and a formant shift sound so different. The interface should help reinforce this distinction by presenting pitch and formant controls in separate sections and by using visual feedback that shows pitch and formant positions as independent dimensions.

Expecting a vocoder to work without a modulator signal is a setup error. The vocoder requires two inputs: a modulator and a carrier. If no modulator signal is present, or if the modulator input is silent, the vocoder produces silence or a static, unmodulated version of the carrier. Users who patch only a carrier and hear no vocoder effect need to understand that the modulator is not optional but is the source of the spectral articulation that defines the vocoder sound.

Neglecting modulator signal quality undermines vocoder intelligibility. A noisy, distant, or reverberant modulator signal produces a smeared spectral envelope that the vocoder applies to the carrier, resulting in muddy, indistinct output. Close-miked, dry vocal signals work best. Preprocessing the modulator with compression to even out its dynamics and gating to remove background noise between phrases significantly improves vocoder clarity and responsiveness.

Overlooking the unvoiced component of speech is a subtler mistake. Consonants like "s," "t," and "f" are produced by turbulent airflow rather than vocal cord vibration. They contain noise-like energy rather than harmonic content. A vocoder that only shapes the carrier using envelope followers may lose these consonant sounds entirely, making the output sound vowel-heavy and mushy. Some vocoders address this with an unvoiced detector that mixes noise into the carrier when the modulator contains non-pitched content, significantly improving the naturalness and clarity of the vocoded speech.

Setting formant filter resonance too high produces an aggressive, ringing quality that overwhelms the source signal rather than shaping it. The formant filters should color the sound, not dominate it. Moderate resonance produces clear vowel peaks without the filters self-oscillating or overshadowing the harmonic content of the source. Users accustomed to sweeping a single resonant filter may not anticipate how multiple high-resonance filters in parallel compound the effect, producing a harsh or unstable output.

Attempting to use formant synthesis at extreme pitch ranges without compensating for the harmonic-to-formant relationship produces poor results. At very low pitches the harmonics are dense enough to fill formant peaks well, but at very high pitches the wide harmonic spacing means formant filters may amplify only a single harmonic or none at all, causing the vowel character to collapse. Users should be aware that formant patches designed in the middle of the keyboard may need formant position adjustment or keyboard tracking when played at extreme registers.

## Design Recommendations For Digital Synth

A vocoder is best implemented as an effect module that can accept an external audio input or an internal signal as the modulator. The carrier should be routable from any synthesis voice or oscillator within the instrument. This architecture integrates the vocoder into the existing signal flow rather than treating it as a standalone tool. The vocoder's band count should be configurable, with sensible defaults around sixteen bands and the option to increase for higher fidelity or decrease for a more synthetic character.

Formant synthesis does not require a dedicated vocoder module. It can be achieved through carefully tuned parallel band-pass filters within the existing filter architecture. If the synthesizer's filter section supports multiple parallel filters with independent frequency and resonance parameters, formant synthesis becomes available as a filter configuration rather than a separate feature. This approach leverages existing infrastructure and makes vocal timbres accessible without adding a new processing module.

Formant positions should be available as modulation destinations within the modulation matrix. Allowing LFOs, envelopes, velocity, aftertouch, or macro controls to drive formant positions means that vowel shapes can evolve over the course of a note, respond to performance gestures, or cycle rhythmically. A dedicated vowel morph parameter that interpolates between stored vowel positions and connects to the modulation matrix would give users intuitive control over vocal animation.

Both vocoding and formant synthesis are later expansions that benefit from a mature filter and effects architecture. The subtractive core must first establish robust band-pass filtering, parallel filter routing, and a flexible effects chain. Once those foundations are in place, vocoder and formant features can be added as configurations or modules that build on existing capabilities rather than requiring new signal processing infrastructure.

Preset design for vocal synthesis should include a library of stored vowel positions based on acoustic research data. Providing accurate F1 and F2 values for common vowels across different vocal types gives users a starting point that produces recognizable results immediately. Users can then modify these positions to taste. Storing vowel presets as named entries that can be assigned to morph endpoints makes formant synthesis approachable even for users unfamiliar with the acoustics of vowel production.

Visualization supports both features. A spectral display showing the modulator's envelope alongside the shaped carrier output helps users understand what the vocoder is doing. A formant map showing vowel positions as points in a two-dimensional space, with F1 on one axis and F2 on the other, makes formant synthesis intuitive and connects the parameter values to recognizable vowel sounds. These visual tools align with the project's goal of helping users understand why a patch sounds the way it does.

The vocoder and formant synthesis features should share underlying filter infrastructure where possible. The band-pass filters used for vocoder bands and the band-pass filters used for formant resonators serve similar roles and can use the same filter algorithms. Designing the filter module to support both fixed-frequency operation (for vocoder bands at predetermined positions) and dynamically tuned operation (for formant synthesis with modulatable positions) ensures that both features benefit from the same filter quality and performance optimizations. This shared foundation reduces the overall design surface and encourages consistency between vocoder and formant behaviors.

An internal modulator option for the vocoder would allow it to function without an external microphone input. By routing another oscillator, a noise source, or even a sample playback engine as the modulator, the vocoder becomes self-contained within the synthesizer. This internal routing broadens the vocoder from a performance tool that requires a live voice input into a sound design tool that can operate entirely within a patch. The modulator source should be selectable between external input and any available internal signal path, making the vocoder flexible enough for both live performance and studio preset design.

The freeze function is a valuable extension for both vocoding and formant synthesis. Freezing the vocoder captures the current spectral envelope from the modulator and holds it indefinitely, allowing the carrier to sustain the frozen spectral shape without a continuous modulator signal. This enables a performer to speak a vowel, freeze the spectral shape, and then play the carrier melodically with that fixed vocal quality. For formant synthesis, a freeze function is implicit in the design since formant positions are already parameter-defined rather than analysis-derived. However, a freeze control on a vocoder adds a performative dimension that bridges the gap between real-time vocal analysis and static formant shaping.
